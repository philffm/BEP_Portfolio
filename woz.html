Week 7/8: "Wizard of Oz" - Prototyping

7.1 Introduction

The next 2 weeks, we are going to work under the motto “Fake it, till you make it”. Nothing particularly new to me, I thought. But as it turned out, the way we do this in terms of prototyping was something not entirely new to me, but definitely an interesting task. To be exact: Our assignment deals with “Wizard of Oz”-prototyping. The cool thing about this kind of prototype was not clear to me in the beginning – but I am happy to explain it: Without the effort of developing a fully featured software, that dynamically reacts on the user’s input, The software is working from the visual perspective, while the functional side is still emulated by a human being.

This still sounds way too abstract to you? Well, let me give a few examples, where such things happened in the past:

  • “The chess Turk” – was a so called chess-“robot”, which consisted both of a checkerboard and a puppet, while the entire functional side and motion were controlled by a human sitting right underneath the checkerboard. However, the audience still perceived as legitimate chess robot.
  • A translation software, that pretended to use a computer-generated voice, but factually used human translators to complete that job.

7.2 Our task: Create a “Wizard of Oz”-Prototype

In a nutshell, we got the assignment to create our own “Wizard of Oz”-Prototype using the following ingredients:

  • Create a visualization / game, that reacts to the user’s input – This could be either achieved using Processing (Java compiler for digital artists / designers), but we are still free to use anything else that could serve this purpose.
  • A piece of software used for “mapping”, that enables us to map our visuals / game to every possible surface (e.g. at both the wall and ground at the same time without facing any distortion effects.
  • Video projector
  • Emphasize in social interaction between the users
  • Hardware/software-supported interaction detection / processing Again- this part is not needed, as it’s a “Wizard of Oz-Prototype” and thus it is part of our manual work as a team: To see, what our users do and react with different functional states .

7.3 Our Wizard of Oz prototype-setup

For this assignment, I formed a team with Justus Bremer, with whom I came to a quick agreement on what kind of prototype to use. As we both created a “catching”-game in Processing before, we started right away with an ideation session on how to include the social interaction of min. 2 players in this game.

Our final concept, although did not have a lot in common with the both games we created before. The ideation session lead us to an entirely new interpretation of this game concept.

The idea:

Instead of catching items falling from the top of the screen, there would be 5 lanes with 3 or 2 items falling from the top. In addition to that, we thought about the aspect on how to include emotions in the social interaction and thus decided to use objects, people connect fears or disgust with.

8.2 Evaluation

When we got the assignment, the actual potential of Wizard of Oz prototyping was not yet entirely clear to me. I assumed, the effort is going to be high, while the outcome will be comparatively low. To my surprise, once I started digging in deeper to the concept of Wizard of Oz prototyping, the whole idea and the value behind it became clear to me .

I started understanding, how testing of prototypes that are already perceived as fully functional by the users is made possible with a comparably low effort. This allows large scale testing already in a very early stage, using simple code without needing to build the fully interactive product.

Thus, I am very sure to make use of this prototyping concept more often from now on.
