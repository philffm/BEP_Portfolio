Week 2: Non-visual prototyping

2.1. Introduction

This week, we were trying out new things. Even though I was aware about the fact we have more than our visual senses and I even did some voice prototyping for voice assistants using a tool called “VoiceX”, I never really thought about how to “sketch” voice prototypes with a low-fidelity, minimalistic approach.

This week’s assignment was meant to emphasize exactly this particular aspect of non-visual low fidelity prototypes applied to the fitness application created last week. To be exact, we had to cover every particular function of the fitness application

  • Start/Stop
  • Drink
  • Faster/Slower
  • Achievements
  • Optional features: (In my case: sleep tracker)

For prototyping we had different options to choose from. As I was sick this week and my voice not really a joy to use, I decided to drive the “Foley” approach, where noises are generated by objects.

2.3. Reflective conclusion: Non-visual prototyping

Another week - another new insight: I never thought that deep on the impact,  sound-feedback has on how users experience an interactive application. I had to realize, that for many applications (apart from slot machines), the involvement of sound-feedback can play a crucial role but is not really practiced - sometimes due to designers not being aware of that need, sometimes through software restrictions, even in prototyping tools with strong high fidelity focus. That's why I was excited, on how easy fitting sounds can be generated in the context of low-fidelity.

A main struggle, I was still not able to fix is how to actually use the audio files in the popular prototyping tools without using advanced web technology or playing the files manually during the usability test.
